Place Value

01

Place value is what we use to read numbers. Each digit or integer has a specific value depending on where it is placed in a number.

02

We have the number 15 (fifteen). This is made up by placing a 1 in the tens column and a 5 in the units column. All numbers have place value, allowing us to accurately interpret their value.

03

The highest integer that we will ever have in each column is 9. If we add another to a column with a 9 in it then it will reset to 0 and the column to the left will increase by 1.

Addition

01

Addition and subtraction are part of the building blocks of Mathematics. Addition is the process of adding to another number or amount. We can then calculate a total, for example- 3 + 2 gives us a total of 5.

02

We can use what we know about place value to help with our addition and subtraction. If we align each place value column within our calculations it will make it easier to complete them.

03

Be aligning our place value columns we can just focus on adding each column one at a time.

Subtraction

01

Subtraction is the process of taking away from or reducing an amount by a specific value. Take 6 - 2 as a basic example. We will again arrive at a total of 4.

02

Again by using what we know about place value we can align our subtraction calculation up in the respective columns to make the calculations much easier for us.

03

There may be times where we cannot complete a particular part on a calculation, in this example the first part of 4 - 6 cannot be done as it would leave us with a negative answer so we look to borrow from the column to the left of this. Borrowing one from the 9 makes this an 8 but it makes the column we are working in a 14 instead of a 4. Now we can complete this by taking 6 away from 14.

Multiplication (Grid)

01

Multiplication is simply a fast method of addition. For example we could do an addition calculation of 4 + 4 + 4 + 4 + 4 or we could do a much quicker calculation of 4 x 5.

02

If we had a sum such as 32 x 26, a common way of working this out would be through using the grid or box method of multiplication. We would split the numbers into their place value representations- so here we would split 32 into 30 and 2, we would then split 26 into a 20 and a 6.

03

We can then multiply each of the numbers together, for example 30 x 20 = 600, 2 x 20 = 40, 30 x 6 = 180 and 2 x 6 = 12.

04

Now that we have the answers to each individual calculation we just need to add these up to final our final answer of 832.

Multiplication (Column)

01

We can also complete multiplication sums using the column method, this utilises our knowledge of place value- we align our sum in respects to its place value columns, for example: 34 x 7 would be set out like this.

02

We have to multiply each number on the top row by the number on the second row individually. So we will start by multiplying 4 x 7 which would give us 28. We would write the 8 in the units column and carry over the 2 into the tens column.

03

We then multiply the 3 by 7 to get 21, but we must then add the 2 onto this that we carried over from the previous step. This would make it 23. We don't need to carry anything over this time as we have reached the end of our multiplication problem. We now have our answer of 238.

Division

01

There are three parts that make up every division calculation- the dividend, the divisor and the quotient. The dividend is the number that is being divided, the divisor is the number that we are dividing the dividend by and the quotient is the result of this division.

02

One of the most common ways of approaching a division sum is to use the bus stop method, using a set of lines at a right-angle to split the sum up. For example- 52 ÷ 4 would look like this.

03

We begin by looking at how many 4's we can divide into the number 5. We know that a single 4 can fit into the number 5 with 1 remaining. We write a 1 on our answer line above the 'bus stop' and also move over the one that was remaining to the next column.

04

Lastly we now have to look at how many 4's we can divide into 12. We know this to be 3 so we can write this above the bus stop and as we don't have anything left over we have now arrived at our answer- a quotient of 13.

Fractions Introduction

01

A fraction is used to represent part (or a number of parts) of a number or quantity. A fractions has two parts, the numerator and denominator. The denominator is the bottom number and tells us how many parts the number or quantity is divided into and the numerator, which is the top number, tells us how many of these parts we have.

02

For example, if we look at a pie that is split up into 8 pieces and we eat 3 of them (as shown in green on the diagram), we can say that we have eaten 3/8 (three eighths) of the pie. You can also see that this leaves 5/8 (five eighths) of the pie still to be eaten (shown in white).

Fractions of an Amount

01

Fractions are an integral part of our daily lives and we will often have to calculate a fractions of a particular value, for example we are wanting to know what three quarters of an hour is.

02

We know that 1 hour is 60 minutes so we can rewrite the question to make it a little easier to us because if we split up an hour it would make more sense to give our answer in minutes.

03

Firstly we need to divide our amount- 60 minutes, by the bottom part of the fraction- thus splitting this amount up into four parts and giving us the value of one of those parts. Once we divide this we have 15 minutes, we now know this is one quarter of 60 minutes but we actually need three quarters.

04

Lastly we take our value from the first part of the calculation- 15 and then multiply it by the top number of the fraction, here this is 3. We now have our final answer of 45 minutes which we have found out is three quarters of an hour. This process will work for any fraction of any amount- just divide your amount by the bottom part of the fraction and then multiply your answer by the top part of the fraction.

Fractions (Simplifying)

01

Sometimes we may be faced with fractions that contain very large numbers, for example 25/65. This can be quite difficult to look at and work with so we can look to simplify this into an equivalent fraction (a fraction that means the same) with smaller numbers.

02

We can simplify the fraction by looking at what is the biggest number that divides evenly into both the top AND bottom part of the fraction, this would be 5 in this case.

03

If we divide both the top and bottom part of the fraction by 5 we get a much simpler fraction of 5/13 to work with. We cannot simplify this anymore because both numbers, in this case, are prime numbers. We must always make sure that when we are simplifying fractions we divide BOTH the top AND bottom part of the fraction by the same number.

Fractions (Multiplying)

01

We can also sometimes be asked to multiply two or more fractions together, thankfully this is an easier process than addition and subtraction with fractions. If we take a the sum 2/5 x 1/6 we can work through the steps needed to complete the calculation.

02

When multiplying fractions all we need to do is multiply the numerators with each other and also the exact same process with the denominators, so here we have 2 x 1 = 2 and 5 x 6 = 30.

03

Fractions (Divide)

01

We may sometimes be asked to divide two or more fractions and thankfully there is an easy way to do this. It is often referred to as the KFC method- with KFC standing for Keep, Flip, Change. If we have a problem such as 3/7 ÷ 3/8 we can use the KFC strategy to work this out. We KEEP the first fraction.

02

We then FLIP the second fraction so that the bottom part of the fraction is now on top and the top part of the fraction is now on the bottom.

03

We then CHANGE the division into a multiplication. So we now have 3/7 x 8/3.

04

We can then use what we know about multiplying fractions to finish the calculation. We multiply the top and bottom numbers to get our answer of 24/21.

Fractions (Mixed Numbers)

01

Mixed numbers are basically numbers mixed with fractions, an example of a mixed number would be 4 and 1/3, which is shown like this.

02

We can convert this into a fraction to help with calculations. Of course when we do we will find that the numerator (top number) is bigger than the denominator (bottom number). This type of fraction is called an improper fraction. We can convert this by multiplying the whole number by the denominator and then adding the numerator to this and putting it over the denominator once again.

03

We can also convert from an improper fraction to a mixed number by asking how many times the denominator divides into the numerator, in this new example of 23/7 we know that 7 divides into 23 three times which is 21. So 3 will be our whole number, leaving 2/7 left over. The mixed number is therefore 3 and 2/7.

Decimals (Place Value)

01

Place value for decimals follows a similar pattern to that of whole numbers however they appear to move in the opposite direction. For example in the decimal number 0.135 we have one tenth, three hundredths and five thousandths that make up this number.

02

We can then use this knowledge to help us with any other calculations that we may need to do with decimal numbers etc.

Decimals (Add & Subtract)

01

We can use what we know about decimal place value to help with any addition and subtraction calculations. A common strategy to use is to line up the decimals in our numbers so the place value columns are also lined up. For example with the sum 1.346 + 2.14.

02

Notice that the two decimal numbers don't have the same amount of digits? Well we can just fill in the gap in the thousandth column with a zero to make them both have the same number of digits.

03

Now we add up each column just like we would with whole numbers- the same process now that we have lined up our decimals. If we do so we will get the answer 3.486.

Decimals (Multiplication)

01

Multiplying decimals can be very difficult at times but one useful strategy is to remove the decimals entirely when carrying out the multiplication. If we had the sum 1.23 x 0.4, we would look at this as 123 x 4.

02

Again, knowing what we have looked at with place value we will complete the calculation as usual. Here 123 x 4 equals 492.

02

Lastly we put the decimals back into our sum and count how many digits are after decimals in our sum, here we have three digits after decimals- the 23 and the 4. We now match make sure our answer also has three digits after a decimal point. We now have our answer of 0.492.

Decimals (Division)

01

It can be difficult when given a division sum involving decimal numbers, for example- 1.44 ÷ 1.2.

02

We can use the bus stop method as we usually would for a division sum and to make our calculations easier we can get rid of the decimal in the 1.2 by multiplying it by 10, this will make it 12. We also have to remember to also multiply our other number by 10 too, now 1.44 becomes 14.4.

03

Now we can complete the calculation as we usually would with a division sum but we must make sure that the decimal point lines up with that in our 14.4, if we do so we will get the answer 1.2. This is the correct answer and we don't need to do any converting back to our original numbers because we multiplied them both by the same power of 10.

Decimals (Ordering)

We can sometimes be asked to order a set of decimal numbers from either largest to smallest or smallest to largest. Say for example the numbers 0.31, 0,3, 0.311, 0.133, 0.13.

02

This can be quite difficult to do when looking at the numbers in a row, it would be easier to compare when listing them in a column as we are used to seeing our numbers with their respective place values. To make all the numbers have the same digits we will add zero's to fill in the gaps.

02

Notice that the beginning of each number is a zero? In this case we don't need to focus on that, now we can just compare the numbers as if they were whole numbers that we are more used to working with. We can then put them in order much more easily.
